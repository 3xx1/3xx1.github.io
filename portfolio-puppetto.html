Puppetto

Overview

Papetto is a lightweight robotic arm that is attachable to anything (such as a phone) and moves according to face detection techniques in order to mirror facial movements such as head shaking, leaning and tilting. Puppetto embodies those mirroring activities as physical movements in order to give a boost to empathy during a conversation between persons in remote places, and enables them to experience new forms of intimacy and connection in remote communication.

My Role

In this project, my main role was a Design Technologist, leading the development for prototypes as well as being engaged in the ideation process. I implemented face recognition as an iOS application, a servo motors control system of the robotic arm via Arduino, and a part of 3D-printing for the robotic arm.

Process

The idea for this project started from hacking everyday objects as Internet of Things (IoT) so that it can enrich conversations between two remote places (e.g., long distance relationship, family who lives outside of the country, etc.). First, we had a brainstorming session in order to figure out how everyday objects can potentially have other channels for additional subtle information. After having this brainstorming session, we decided on a mobile phone, which is now probably one of the most common and the most frequently used everyday objects in our life.

Since we also wanted to specifically focus on enhancing the communication experience, we decided to create an attachment to a phone, which can offer a richer mode of communication while having a conversation between remote places. We finalized our idea to make the phone embody physical gestures on the other side as physical movements, like head shaking, leaning and titling.

We prototyped the concept as a robotic arm that is attachable to a phone while video chatting, and embodies gestures on the other side. My role was to work on facial gesture recognition through image processing (openFrameworks) and its connection with servo motors (Arduino). In addition, I also made a part of the robotic arm with Rhinoceros and MakerBot.


Outcome

We finished the first prototype and published the project in the HRI conference Proceedings. We are also going to present it at the demo session, and we are still continuing on the development.
